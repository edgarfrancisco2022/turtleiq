---
title: On Learning Slowly
date: 2026-04-03
---

There is a certain pressure in modern learning culture to go fast. Finish the course. Complete the curriculum. Move on to the next thing.

I've spent a lot of time trying to learn quickly, and I've spent a lot of time forgetting quickly too. The two seem to go together.

At some point I started paying more attention to what I actually retained versus what I had merely passed through. The gap was uncomfortable. I had read hundreds of articles, finished dozens of books, and taken several courses — and when I tried to reconstruct what I knew, most of it was gone. Not all of it. But most of it.

The things I remembered were the ones I had thought about enough to form a clear, personal representation of. A short sentence. A vivid example. A connection to something I already knew well. The material that stuck was the material I had made my own.

That observation led to a shift in how I approach learning. Instead of optimizing for throughput — how many things I expose myself to — I started optimizing for depth of encoding on a smaller set of things. Fewer concepts, understood better.

The metaphor I keep coming back to is the difference between a tourist and someone who has lived somewhere. A tourist can visit twenty cities in a summer. Someone who has lived in a city knows where to get a good breakfast on a Sunday morning. The knowledge has a different quality to it.

I'm not arguing against breadth. Broad exposure matters — it creates the raw material that deep understanding can later process. But breadth without consolidation is expensive. You pay the cost of attention without collecting the return.

The practice I've settled into is simple: when I encounter something worth keeping, I slow down enough to write a minimal representation of it in my own words. Not a summary of the source. My own formulation. The version that makes sense to me, using examples I can picture.

It takes longer. But the things I write down this way, I actually know.
