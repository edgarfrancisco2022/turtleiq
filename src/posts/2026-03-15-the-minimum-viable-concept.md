---
title: The Minimum Viable Concept
date: 2026-03-15
---

When I first started building TortugaIQ, I had to answer a question I hadn't thought carefully about before: what is the smallest unit of knowledge worth storing?

My initial instinct was to store things the way I encountered them — articles, chapters, lecture notes. But those are documents, not knowledge. They contain knowledge, but the knowledge is diffuse inside them. Retrieving something from a document means reading the document again.

I wanted something more atomic. Something I could look at in isolation and immediately know what it meant.

I landed on what I now call the MVK — Minimum Viable Knowledge. For any concept I want to retain, the MVK is the smallest representation of it that is still genuinely useful. Not a definition copied from Wikipedia. Not a summary of a chapter. My own formulation: the version that captures what I actually understood, in the form that would be most useful to me during a review session six months from now.

For some concepts, the MVK is a single sentence. For others, it's a short list of properties, or a simple example, or a sketch of a relationship between two ideas.

The constraint matters. Forcing myself to write something minimal means I have to make a choice about what the core of the concept actually is. That choice is itself a learning act. It's harder than copying text. It requires that I understand the thing well enough to compress it.

There's a related idea in software: the minimum viable product. The discipline isn't about doing less — it's about identifying what the essential thing is, before adding anything on top of it. A minimum viable product that doesn't solve the core problem isn't minimum or viable. Similarly, an MVK that is too vague, too compressed, or too dependent on context isn't really a knowledge representation — it's a placeholder.

Getting the MVK right is the most cognitively demanding part of the workflow. It's also the part that pays off most during review.

When I open a concept and see an MVK I wrote three months ago, I'm not reading someone else's summary. I'm reading my own understanding, compressed to its essence. That is a very different kind of recall.
